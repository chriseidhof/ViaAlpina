R29 Seekofelhütte / Rifugio Biella » St. Martin in Gsies / S. Martino in Casies
  |   4h10   |   26.5 km   |   54 m   |   1146 m

Climb downhill eastward over the Törlscharte ridge near the Seekofel Hut (2,327m) to the "Nabiges Loch“ valley floor. Then continue to the Pragser Wildsee lake at approx. 1,500m; this brings you to a section of the dolomites high altitude trail no. 1. Now, follow a 12km route over the level terrain of the Pragser valley to Welsberg. From here, you can take the regular bus into the Gsieser Tal Valley.

Detailed route description
Walk over the “Törl”, a pass with a wayside shrine (2,388m) to the east below the Ofenmauer to reach the ravine-like Nabige Loch valley and then continue northward to reach the Prags Wildsee lake, which lies at the feet of the 1,400 metre-high north face of the Seekofel. The water at approx. 1,500m still harbours the legend of the drowned kingdom of the Fanes kings. From the pass to the lake, there is a descent of around 900 metres in altitude. Care must be taken around the Nabige Loch area at approx. 2,000m, as the trail becomes rather narrow in parts. Until this point, the hike constantly takes the route of dolomite ridge trail no. 1. Next, the hiking trail leads around the left shore of the lake past the Prags Wildsee hotel (the first accommodation possibility since the Seekofel Hut) out into Prags valley, which veers southward from the upper Pustertal valley between the villages of Welsberg and Niederdorf and which (seen from the north) splits after Außerprags into a western and an eastern arm. Keep following the markings for trail no. 1 on a 6km walk partly through lush high-alpine forest to reach Schmieden, the main town. After this comes another 6km on Shanks’s pony, alternating between woodland and level forest or asphalt roads, before coming to a rather monotonous stretch of the trail (no. 2a) to reach the community of Welsberg with 2,500 inhabitants (approx. 1,090m) in the Pustertal valley. From here, the day’s stage can be shortened by using the regular bus service and heading to St. Martin (1,276m) in the Gsieser valley. In the section from the Prags Wildsee lake to the Pustertal valley, use of a map is recommended as the route is not obvious and the trail type changes constantly.
On reaching Welsberg, there is a difficult walk from the village centre on trail no. 2a to the opposite side of the valley.
(Stefan Illmer, Karin Leichter, AVS)
Natural and cultural heritage
On this stage, Via Alpina leaves the dolomites behind and leads into the mountains north of the Pustertal valley. From a geological angle, you are therefore crossing the border between the central and southern alps, the so-called “Pustertal line“. Overall, this dividing line is referred to as the Periadriatic line and represents a significant seismic line. The Fanes-Sennes-Prags nature park offers nature lovers an inexhaustible wealth of natural monuments whose preservation should be of importance to all. The park in the “kingdom of the pale mountains” owes its great charm to the bizarre rock formations of the peaks and also to the most beautiful natural lakes in South Tyrol and extremely rich and interesting flora and fauna. Embedded in the area of the Fanes-Sennes-Prags nature park and in the unique mountain world of the Prags dolomites lies Prags valley, opening out towards the south. The administrative centre is Schmieden. Today, a traditional farming locality with the three separate communities of Außerprags, Innerprags and St. Veit lives in a natural landscape with an incomparable plant and animal world. Mighty dolomite peaks offering numerous hiking and climbing tours of all levels of difficulty rise up between the water of the Wildsee lake that closes off the valley and the mountain meadows in the Plätzwiese holiday resort. The alpine terrain is therefore an inviting place for mountaintop experiences and panoramic vantage points. Churches and chapels are an important component in Tyrolean culture. As it was the custom in earlier days to erect at least one chapel in each hamlet, numerous religious buildings are situated in the surrounding area. In Prags alone there are two churches and seven chapels. The Pustertal valley is the third biggest valley community in South Tyrol and plays a significant role in cultural and business life. This diagonal alpine valley runs along the south side of the main alpine ridge for about 60km in an east-west direction and begins (on Italian territory) at Innichen, then continuing westward until reaching the Brixen basin. Due to the high-altitude location (climbing from 750m above sea level), there are no very densely populated areas. The landscape is thus predominantly covered by meadows, arable land and above all large coniferous forests. These are interrupted by villages and settlements and scattered mountain farmsteads. The Bruneck basin accommodating the main town of the Pustertal valley is an exception, and represents the economic and cultural centre of this countryside area. As in many other alpine valleys, tourism is the most important economic sector in the Pustertal.
(Stefan Illmer, Karin Leichter, AVS)
Other long-distance trails and alternative routes
The dolomites high altitude trail no. 1 leads from the Pragser Wildsee lake in 8 daily stages to Belluno (marked with a blue triangle bearing the number 1), also taking in the Seekofel mountains. We take the trail section in the opposite direction, i.e. from the Seekofel Hut over the pass down to the Nabiges Loch and on until reaching the northern end of the Pragser Wildsee.
